ABOUT:
This metric measures the share of turnover from products/services that carry information on environmental and social parameters relevant to the product. It is a BRSR Principle 9 Essential Indicator under consumer responsibility, linked to product transparency and responsible consumption; ICAI guidance frames this as part of the requirement to disclose turnover carrying information on environmental/social parameters, safe usage, and recycling/safe disposal. A higher value indicates better performance.

PERFORMANCE_AND_DRIVERS:
The benchmark shows a wide disclosure-coverage gap rather than a simple steelmaking-performance gap. JSW reported 100% across FY2023-FY2025, while SAIL reported 100% in FY2023 and FY2024 before falling to 0% in FY2025; Jindal Stainless moved from 0% in FY2023 to 100% in FY2024 and FY2025. Tata Steel remained partial at 50% in FY2023, 53% in FY2024, and 52% in FY2025. Jindal’s FY2025 position is supported by product-level disclosures, customer education, product brochures, technical visits, fabricator training, display vans, and labels carrying grade, batch, heat number, dimensions, certification logos, REACH/RoHS references and BIS/ISI marks for multiple stainless grades.

TATA_POSITIONING:
In FY2024-25, Tata ranks 3rd at 52%, behind JSW and Jindal at 100%; the gap to the best performer is 48 percentage points, or 48% below the 100% benchmark. Tata’s rank improved from 3rd in FY2023 to neither leading nor catching up in FY2024 despite a numeric rise to 53%, because Jindal moved to 100%; Tata then remained 3rd in FY2025 as SAIL fell to 0%. The underlying issue appears to be disclosure coverage and product-information mapping rather than absence of product responsibility systems, because Tata reports value-chain policy coverage, ResponsibleSteel, BIS, ISO and related certifications, and sustainable procurement practices, but these do not translate into 100% turnover coverage for this specific P9 product-information metric.

TARGETS:
No targets identified for this exact metric. Related BRSR commitments include Tata Steel’s Net Zero by 2045 ambition, low-emission EAF transition, and scrap-use expansion, while Jindal Stainless discloses a 50% carbon-emission-intensity reduction target by 2035 versus FY2022 baseline, Net Zero by 2050, 100% supplier ESG assessment ambition, and ResponsibleSteel certification ambition.

COMPARABILITY_NOTES:
Tata Steel FY2022-23 is flagged as consolidated in the standardised dataset, while later Tata figures include restatements and boundary changes linked to mergers and SEBI methodology updates. SAIL’s FY2025 fall from 100% to 0% should be read as a disclosure/classification movement unless corroborated by a detailed methodology note, not as proof of a sudden product-portfolio change.

ABOUT:
This metric measures the share of turnover from products/services that carry information on recycling and/or safe disposal. It is a BRSR Principle 9 Essential Indicator because it relates to consumer information on reuse, recycling, and safe end-of-life handling. A higher value indicates better performance.

PERFORMANCE_AND_DRIVERS:
JSW reported full 100% coverage in all three years, while SAIL reported 100% in FY2023 and FY2024 before dropping to 0% in FY2025. Jindal Stainless improved sharply from 4.1% in FY2023 to 100% in FY2024 and FY2025, supported by stainless steel’s circularity proposition: JSL states that end-of-life stainless steel scrap is not treated as waste but as a valuable input for remelting, while safe disposal is managed through authorised recyclers. Tata Steel reported 19% in FY2023, 16% in FY2024, and 17% in FY2025, despite strong underlying circularity actions: in FY2024-25 Tata recycled about 4.2 million tonnes of scrap, including approximately 1.7 million tonnes internal and 2.5 million tonnes external scrap.

TATA_POSITIONING:
In FY2024-25, Tata ranks 3rd at 17%, behind JSW and Jindal at 100%; the gap to the best performer is 83 percentage points, or 83% below the 100% benchmark. Tata’s trajectory is mixed: it worsened from 19% to 16% in FY2024, then improved marginally to 17% in FY2025, but did not close the structural gap to full-coverage reporters. This is an important positioning issue because Tata has substantive circular-economy capability through scrap use, IBMD by-product valorisation, and Steel Recycling Business, but the consumer-facing turnover coverage on recycling/safe-disposal information remains low relative to peers.

TARGETS:
No targets identified for this exact recycling/safe-disposal product-information coverage metric. Related targets include Tata Steel Nederland’s aim to increase scrap usage from 17% in 2019 to 30% by 2030 and Tata Steel UK’s EAF transition, expected to increase scrap usage from 17% to over 70%; Jindal Stainless is working toward Zero-Waste-to-Landfill certification by 2030 and promotes a closed-loop recycling system.

COMPARABILITY_NOTES:
This indicator measures information coverage as a percentage of turnover, not actual recycling rate, scrap charge, by-product utilisation, or waste diversion. Tata’s low value therefore should not be read as low circularity performance; it reflects that only 17% of FY2024-25 turnover carried the relevant recycling/safe-disposal information under the standardised dataset.

ABOUT:
This metric measures the number of dealers/distributors through whom sales are made under the BRSR “Open-ness of business” disclosure on concentration of sales. It is a transparency and concentration indicator, associated with governance and market-access disclosure rather than a Principle 9 product-information Essential Indicator. A higher value indicates better performance because it suggests broader channel reach and lower reliance on a narrow dealer/distributor base.

PERFORMANCE_AND_DRIVERS:
All companies were missing this datapoint in FY2023 and therefore rank last under the stated rule. In FY2024, Tata led with 1,176 dealers/distributors, followed by JSW at 296, Jindal at 240, and SAIL at 194. In FY2025, Tata expanded further to 1,661, Jindal rose to 367, JSW declined to 270, and SAIL declined to 168. Tata’s scale and distributor architecture are visible in its FY2025 openness disclosure: standalone sales to dealers/distributors were 38% of total sales, with 1,661 dealers/distributors, and Tata clarifies that its channels are direct sales and distributor sales. Jindal’s FY2025 dealer/distributor base increased alongside dealer/distributor sales at 32% of total sales, while SAIL’s dealer/distributor sales were 15.91% with 168 dealers/distributors.

TATA_POSITIONING:
In FY2024-25, Tata ranks 1st with 1,661 dealers/distributors. The closest peer is Jindal Stainless at 367, which is 77.9% behind Tata on count; Tata’s network is about 4.5x Jindal’s, 6.2x JSW’s, and 9.9x SAIL’s. Tata’s trajectory is clearly positive: from missing disclosure in FY2023 to rank 1 in FY2024 and a further 41% increase in FY2025. This supports Tata’s market-access positioning, especially for distributor-led domestic reach, but it should be read together with sales concentration and top-10 distributor share rather than as a standalone quality metric.

TARGETS:
No targets identified for this metric.

COMPARABILITY_NOTES:
Absolute dealer/distributor count is affected by company scale, channel model, product mix, and whether the company defines distributors, dealers, and stockists similarly. FY2023 values are missing for all companies and therefore all are ranked last by rule, but FY2023 should not be used for substantive peer differentiation on this indicator.

Jindal: FY2023-24=Improved, FY2024-25=Improved

ABOUT:
This metric measures the number of trading houses from which purchases are made under the BRSR “Open-ness of business” disclosure on concentration of purchases. It is a transparency and procurement-concentration indicator, not a Principle 9 consumer Essential Indicator. A lower value indicates better performance because fewer or zero trading-house purchases generally implies more direct procurement and lower intermediary dependence, when read alongside purchases-from-trading-houses percentage.

PERFORMANCE_AND_DRIVERS:
All companies had missing FY2023 values and therefore rank last for that year. In FY2024 and FY2025, Tata and SAIL rank best with zero trading houses, while JSW reduced from 33 to 26 and Jindal reduced from 500 to 478. Tata’s FY2025 BRSR explicitly reports 0% purchases from trading houses, zero trading houses, and zero top-10 trading-house concentration, noting that this follows the SEBI industry-standard definition based on available company data. SAIL similarly reports Nil purchases from trading houses and “Not Applicable” for number and top-10 share. Jindal’s high count is structurally linked to its stainless value chain: FY2025 purchases from trading houses were 80% of total purchases, from 478 trading houses, with top-10 trading houses accounting for 62% of purchases from trading houses.

TATA_POSITIONING:
In FY2024-25, Tata is tied rank 1 with SAIL at zero trading houses. Because the best value is zero, a percentage gap is not mathematically meaningful; on an absolute basis, Tata is 26 trading houses better than JSW and 478 better than Jindal. Tata’s trajectory moved from missing in FY2023 to best-in-class in FY2024 and remained stable in FY2025. The key positioning message is procurement-route clarity: Tata’s openness disclosure indicates no trading-house dependence under the SEBI industry-standard definition, while Jindal’s stainless-steel and scrap/alloy-linked sourcing model creates much higher trading-house exposure.

TARGETS:
No targets identified for this metric. Related procurement commitments include Tata’s Responsible Supply Chain Policy and Sustainable Procurement Framework, and Jindal’s target to assess 100% of suppliers on ESG criteria.

COMPARABILITY_NOTES:
This count must be read with “purchases from trading houses as % of total purchases.” A company could have many trading houses but low purchase concentration, or few trading houses but high dependence; in this dataset, Jindal has both a high number and high purchase share from trading houses. FY2023 values are missing for all companies and are ranked last by rule, but they do not support substantive peer comparison.

FY2022-23:
FY2022-23 leadership is split. On product-information coverage, JSW and SAIL are the clear leaders, each reporting 100% for both environmental/social product information and recycling/safe-disposal information. Tata Steel is mid-pack at 50% and 19%, while Jindal Stainless is the laggard at 0% and 4.1%. The dealer/distributor and trading-house metrics do not differentiate peers in FY2022-23 because all values are missing and therefore ranked last under the defined rule. Tata’s FY2022-23 values also require caution because the dataset flags them as consolidated, including international operations, whereas most other company-years are standalone Indian operations. Overall, SAIL and JSW lead the theme quantitatively for FY2022-23, but this appears driven by product-information coverage methodology rather than necessarily superior product circularity. Jindal’s lag is disclosure-driven, especially given stainless steel’s inherent recyclability, which becomes visible in later-year disclosures.

FY2023-24:
FY2023-24 shows a more balanced but bifurcated benchmark. JSW, SAIL, and Jindal Stainless all report 100% coverage for both product environmental/social information and recycling/safe-disposal information, while Tata remains materially below the leaders at 53% and 16%. However, Tata leads the openness-of-business channel indicators, with 1,176 dealers/distributors and zero trading houses, indicating broad sales reach and no trading-house purchase dependence under the reported definition. Jindal reaches 100% product-information coverage and 240 dealers/distributors, but its procurement model shows high intermediary exposure, with 500 trading houses and 78% purchases from trading houses. SAIL combines 100% product-information coverage with zero trading-house purchases, but has a smaller dealer/distributor base at 194. Therefore, product-responsibility disclosure leaders are JSW, SAIL, and Jindal, while Tata leads market-channel breadth and procurement-route simplicity. The year also marks Tata’s transition into more comparable disclosure after restatements and boundary changes.

FY2024-25:
FY2024-25 is the primary benchmark year. Tata is strongest on channel reach and procurement transparency: it reports 1,661 dealers/distributors, 38% sales through dealers/distributors, and zero trading houses for purchases. JSW and Jindal lead the two product-information indicators at 100% each, while Tata remains materially behind at 52% for environmental/social product information and 17% for recycling/safe-disposal information. SAIL’s product-information indicators fall to 0%, but it retains zero trading-house dependence and reports 168 dealers/distributors. Jindal is the most improved integrated product-responsibility performer, combining 100% product-information coverage with 367 dealers/distributors and strong stainless-specific circularity claims, but it remains the weakest on trading-house dependence: 478 trading houses, 80% purchases from trading houses, and 62% top-10 trading-house concentration. For Tata senior management, the key gap is not circular-economy capability—Tata recycled about 4.2 Mt scrap in FY2024-25—but translation of that capability into full turnover-linked consumer/product information coverage.
