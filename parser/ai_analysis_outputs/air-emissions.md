ABOUT:
SOx emissions measure sulphur oxide emissions, primarily driven by sulphur in fuel, coke, coal and process gases. This is disclosed under BRSR Principle 6, Essential Indicator for air emissions other than GHG emissions. A lower value indicates better performance.

PERFORMANCE_AND_DRIVERS:
Across the three-year series, Jindal Stainless was the lowest SOx emitter in FY2022-23 and FY2023-24, at 0.85189 kt and 3.0721 kt respectively, reflecting the smaller stainless-steel operating footprint and EAF-heavy process route versus larger BF-BOF integrated carbon-steel producers. In FY2024-25, SAIL became the lowest reported SOx emitter at 0.95 kt, while Jindal rose to 4.5807 kt, Tata Steel rose to 46 kt, and JSW was marginally higher at 46.1314 kt. Tata’s SOx moved from 51 kt in FY2022-23 consolidated to 38 kt in FY2023-24, then worsened to 46 kt in FY2024-25. Tata’s FY2025 BRSR notes that capex includes investments for SOx, NOx and dust reduction, while its air-quality mitigation relies on advanced pollution-control technologies, real-time emissions monitoring, upgraded equipment, new technologies and maintenance.

TATA_POSITIONING:
In FY2024-25, Tata ranked 3rd on SOx, behind SAIL and Jindal Stainless, and ahead of JSW by a very narrow margin. Tata’s 46 kt was 45.05 kt higher than SAIL’s 0.95 kt, or approximately 4,742% above the best performer. Tata’s rank moved from 3rd in FY2022-23 to 4th in FY2023-24 and back to 3rd in FY2024-25. The trajectory is therefore mixed: absolute SOx improved in FY2023-24, but worsened in FY2024-25, likely reflecting higher integrated steel operating intensity, BF-BOF route exposure, captive energy and process-gas combustion effects, partially offset by ongoing air-emission control investments.

No targets identified for this metric. Tata discloses investments and mitigation initiatives for SOx reduction, but no quantified SOx target was identified in the BRSR.

ABOUT:
NOx emissions measure nitrogen oxides from high-temperature combustion, reheating, power generation, coke ovens, sinter/pellet operations and other thermal processes. This is disclosed under BRSR Principle 6, Essential Indicator for air emissions other than GHG emissions. A lower value indicates better performance.

PERFORMANCE_AND_DRIVERS:
Jindal Stainless led in FY2022-23 and FY2023-24 with 1.9807 kt and 1.7826 kt respectively, while SAIL led in FY2024-25 at 0.8 kt. Tata reduced NOx from 30 kt in FY2022-23 consolidated to 21 kt in FY2023-24, but increased to 24 kt in FY2024-25; JSW improved steadily from 28.7385 kt to 27 kt and then 25.845 kt. Jindal worsened in FY2024-25 to 2.5275 kt, but remained structurally below integrated BF-BOF peers due to scale and route differences. Tata’s FY2025 BRSR highlights real-time monitoring, upgraded pollution-control equipment, new technologies and maintenance as the core air-quality controls, while the BRSR capex disclosure explicitly includes investments in NOx reduction.

TATA_POSITIONING:
In FY2024-25, Tata ranked 3rd on NOx. Tata’s 24 kt was 23.2 kt higher than SAIL’s best-in-peer value of 0.8 kt, or approximately 2,900% above the best performer. Tata’s rank held at 3rd across FY2022-23, FY2023-24 and FY2024-25, although the absolute trend improved first and then worsened. The key issue is not relative rank deterioration, but the wide absolute gap to SAIL and Jindal in FY2024-25, indicating that route, operating boundary, scale and combustion intensity dominate the peer comparison.

ABOUT:
Particulate Matter measures dust and fine particulate emissions from material handling, sintering, pelletising, coke making, BF/BOF/EAF operations, captive power and fugitive sources. This is disclosed under BRSR Principle 6, Essential Indicator for air emissions other than GHG emissions. A lower value indicates better performance.

PERFORMANCE_AND_DRIVERS:
Jindal Stainless led every year on PM, with 2.0841 kt in FY2022-23, 1.3133 kt in FY2023-24 and 1.6015 kt in FY2024-25. Tata improved consistently from 11 kt consolidated in FY2022-23 to 9 kt in FY2023-24 and 8 kt in FY2024-25, becoming the second-best performer in the latest year. JSW improved from 10.143 kt to 8.62 kt but worsened to 10.84 kt in FY2024-25, while SAIL improved from 11.62 kt in FY2023-24 to 10.7352 kt in FY2024-25. Tata’s improvement aligns with the company’s stated modernisation, upgraded pollution-control equipment and significant reduction in stack dust emissions in India; Jindal also discloses APCDs, fugitive-emission management and continuous air-quality monitoring as mitigation measures.

TATA_POSITIONING:
In FY2024-25, Tata ranked 2nd on PM, behind Jindal Stainless. Tata’s 8 kt was 6.3985 kt higher than Jindal’s 1.6015 kt, or approximately 400% above the best performer. Tata’s ranking improved from 3rd in FY2022-23 and FY2023-24 to 2nd in FY2024-25. This is Tata’s strongest air-emissions signal in the set: despite integrated BF-BOF scale, dust performance improved year-on-year, likely reflecting the impact of stack-dust control, dedusting, maintenance discipline and modernised pollution-control assets.

BENCHMARK: air-emissions

FY2022-23:
Jindal Stainless was the clear leader across the air-emissions theme in FY2022-23, ranking 1st on SOx, NOx and PM. Its values were 0.85189 kt SOx, 1.9807 kt NOx and 2.0841 kt PM, structurally far below Tata Steel and JSW. This reflects both scale and process-route differences: Jindal’s stainless-steel operations are smaller and more EAF/scrap-linked, while Tata and JSW operate larger integrated carbon-steel systems with higher sinter, coke, blast furnace, captive power and process-gas combustion exposure. JSW ranked 2nd across the three metrics, while Tata ranked 3rd across the three metrics. Tata FY2022-23 must be interpreted cautiously because the standardised table flags it as consolidated, including international operations, rather than being directly comparable to standalone Indian operations. SAIL was the laggard by ranking rule because SOx, NOx and PM were not reported in the standardised table for FY2022-23.

FY2023-24:
Jindal Stainless remained the overall leader in FY2023-24, ranking 1st across SOx, NOx and PM. SAIL emerged as a meaningful mid-table performer where data became available, ranking 2nd on SOx and NOx, although it ranked 4th on PM. Tata improved in absolute terms on all three pollutants: SOx declined from 51 kt to 38 kt, NOx from 30 kt to 21 kt, and PM from 11 kt to 9 kt. However, Tata’s peer rank remained weak: 4th on SOx, 3rd on NOx and 3rd on PM. JSW was close to Tata on SOx and PM but weaker on NOx. For Tata, the year indicates operational improvement but insufficient relative outperformance because the peer set includes smaller EAF/stainless operations and SAIL’s lower reported SOx/NOx values. Tata FY2023-24 BRSR also notes revised FY2022-23 numbers due to boundary and methodology changes, so trend interpretation should focus on standardised values rather than raw annual-report figures.

FY2024-25:
FY2024-25 shows a split benchmark. SAIL led SOx and NOx with 0.95 kt and 0.8 kt respectively, while Jindal Stainless led PM with 1.6015 kt and remained second on SOx and NOx. Tata’s strongest result was PM: it improved to 8 kt and ranked 2nd, ahead of SAIL and JSW. Tata’s weaker results were SOx and NOx, where it ranked 3rd with 46 kt and 24 kt respectively, materially above SAIL and Jindal. JSW was the laggard in FY2024-25 overall, ranking 4th on SOx, NOx and PM. The key management takeaway for Tata is that dust controls appear to be delivering relative improvement, but gaseous pollutants remain scale- and route-intensive. Tata’s BRSR indicates relevant capex for SOx, NOx and dust reduction, plus real-time monitoring and upgraded air-pollution-control systems; the next step for competitiveness is translating those controls into sustained SOx/NOx reductions while maintaining the PM improvement trajectory.
