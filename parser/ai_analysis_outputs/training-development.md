ABOUT:
This metric measures the percentage of employees trained on health and safety measures under BRSR Principle 3, Essential Indicator 8. SEBI guidance clarifies that health and safety training can include general H&S training, work-related hazard training, hazardous activity/situation training, and mental health training. A higher value indicates better performance.

PERFORMANCE_AND_DRIVERS:
Tata Steel, JSW Steel and Jindal Stainless reached 100% employee H&S training coverage in FY2024-25, while SAIL declined to 15%, making SAIL the clear laggard in latest-year coverage. Across the three-year period, Tata and JSW were consistently at 100%; Jindal moved from 100% in FY2022-23 to 50.04% in FY2023-24 and back to 100% in FY2024-25; SAIL moved from 30.4% to 32.21% and then down to 15%. Tata’s 100% coverage sits within a broader ISO 45001:2018-based EHS management system with risk identification, safety visits, line walks, fatality risk control, and job-cycle checks. Jindal’s FY2024-25 recovery is supported by a formal safety programme including HIRA, HAZOP, work permits, JSA, toolbox training, SOPs, PPE adherence and a Safety Training Centre. SAIL has ISO 45001:2018 across plants and treats employee training and development as an opportunity aligned to modernization and digital transformation, but coverage conversion into the BRSR training table remains materially weaker than peers.

TATA_POSITIONING:
In FY2024-25, Tata Steel is Rank 1 with 100% employee H&S training coverage. The gap from the best performer is 0 percentage points because Tata is tied with JSW and Jindal; SAIL is 85 percentage points behind Tata. Tata’s ranking has held steady at Rank 1 across FY2022-23, FY2023-24 and FY2024-25, indicating a mature and consistently captured safety training system rather than a one-year compliance spike.

FY2022-23:
FY2022-23 shows a broad leadership cluster rather than a single leader. Tata Steel, JSW Steel and Jindal Stainless reported 100% coverage across employee H&S, employee skill, worker H&S and worker skill-upgradation. SAIL matched the leaders only on worker H&S at 100%, but lagged on employee H&S at 30.4%, employee skill at 32.6%, and did not report worker skill-upgradation. Tata’s FY2022-23 figures are flagged as consolidated, so the 100% coverage includes a wider operating boundary than standalone Indian operations; this makes Tata’s result strong, but not fully like-for-like against standalone peers. The strategic signal is that Tata and JSW appear to have institutionalised training coverage as a system metric, while SAIL’s strength was more concentrated in worker H&S, consistent with safety-critical integrated steel operations and contractor risk management. Jindal’s 100% across all four metrics in FY2022-23 also indicates robust coverage before the FY2023-24 dip.

FY2023-24:
FY2023-24 is the most differentiated year. Tata and JSW remained the benchmark with 100% across all four metrics. SAIL improved employee skill coverage to 50.04% and employee H&S to 32.21%, but still trailed materially and continued to report no worker skill-upgradation value. Jindal Stainless showed a major coverage break: employee H&S at 50.04%, employee skill at 32.21%, worker H&S at 19.27%, and worker skill at 3.73%. The Jindal movement is likely a reporting-boundary, classification or programme-capture issue rather than a structural absence of safety systems, because its later FY2024-25 BRSR describes formal HIRA, HAZOP, JSA, work permits, toolbox training and a Safety Training Centre. Tata’s FY2023-24 disclosure is especially mature because it links 100% coverage to a structured development architecture: foundational training, functional and technical capability, business and leadership skills, digital, sustainability and agility, and worker-specific capability-building models.

FY2024-25:
FY2024-25 is the primary benchmark year and Tata Steel is at the top of the peer set across all four metrics. Tata reports 100% coverage for employees and workers on both H&S and skill upgradation. JSW also reports 100% across all four metrics in the standardised table, and Jindal recovers to 100% across all four metrics. SAIL remains mixed: 100% worker H&S coverage, but only 15% employee H&S, 34% employee skill, and no reported worker skill-upgradation coverage. The key insight for Tata senior management is that Tata’s advantage is not only coverage but consistency at scale: 75,377 employees and 197,585 workers were reported in FY2024-25 training tables, all at 100% training coverage. The main competitive watchpoint is not JSW/Jindal matching Tata numerically at 100%, but whether their disclosures show comparable depth in capability architecture, role-based training, and shop-floor risk controls. Jindal’s FY2024-25 recovery is backed by extensive programme descriptions, while SAIL’s material-issue narrative confirms intent but not comparable coverage conversion.
