ABOUT:
This metric captures the number of complaints reported under the Sexual Harassment of Women at Workplace (Prevention, Prohibition and Redressal) Act, 2013. In the BRSR framework, this is reported under Principle 5, Essential Indicator 7, though it is relevant to the Ethics & Compliance theme because it tests workplace conduct, grievance access, investigation integrity, and non-retaliation systems. A lower value indicates better performance.

PERFORMANCE_AND_DRIVERS:
On the standardised dataset, Tata Steel reported the highest POSH complaint count in every year: 38 in FY2023, 20 in FY2024 and 43 in FY2025, versus JSW at 0, 1 and 7; SAIL at 8, 3 and 3; and Jindal Stainless at 0, 0 and 5. The apparent best performers on raw count are JSW/Jindal in FY2023, Jindal in FY2024 and SAIL in FY2025. However, raw complaint counts need cautious interpretation: for large integrated BF-BOF businesses with mines, downstream facilities, contract workforce interfaces and multi-site grievance systems, higher reported cases may reflect a larger workforce and stronger reporting access, not only higher incidence. Tata’s own FY2025 BRSR shows a mature reporting architecture: Speak Up, Ethics Department investigation, escalation to the Chief Ethics Counsellor or Audit Committee Chairperson, and regular Board/Audit Committee updates; Tata also reports POSH data under reasonable assurance for standalone FY2025. Jindal Stainless disclosed five FY2025 POSH complaints, two pending at year-end but resolved on April 30, 2025, and describes confidentiality, DEI policy coverage and POSH/whistle-blower mechanisms. SAIL moved from seven POSH complaints in FY2023 to three in FY2024, and disclosed confidentiality, counselling/legal support, fair investigation, training and whistle-blower protection mechanisms.

TATA_POSITIONING:
In FY2025, Tata ranks 4th with 43 reported POSH complaints, while the best performer on raw count is SAIL with 3; Tata’s count is 1,333% higher than the best performer. Tata’s trajectory is mixed: it improved from 38 in FY2023 to 20 in FY2024, then worsened sharply to 43 in FY2025. The FY2024 improvement coincided with Tata’s standalone reporting basis in the standardised table, while FY2023 was flagged as consolidated. The FY2025 deterioration is likely driven by a combination of scale, stronger grievance accessibility and a more active ethics/POSH reporting ecosystem, rather than necessarily a pure deterioration in workplace culture. For senior management, the more useful diagnostic is not only reducing total cases but reducing recurrence, improving closure timeliness, strengthening IC consistency across sites, and monitoring cases per female employee/worker.

Tata Steel FY2022-23 is flagged as consolidated in the standardised table, while all other company-years are standalone; this weakens direct comparability for Tata’s FY2023 trend. The standardised table shows Tata FY2024 as 20, while Tata’s FY2025 BRSR prior-year standalone table shows 23; this analysis uses the standardised dataset as instructed. Raw complaint counts are not normalised by female employee/worker base, site count, or maturity of reporting channels, so zero or low counts should not automatically be read as stronger culture.

ABOUT:
This metric captures the number of POSH complaints upheld after inquiry, i.e., complaints where the internal process substantiated the complaint. In the BRSR framework, it is part of Principle 5, Essential Indicator 7, alongside total POSH complaints and complaints as a percentage of female employees/workers. A lower value indicates better performance.

PERFORMANCE_AND_DRIVERS:
FY2023 upheld data is missing for all four peers in the standardised table, so all are ranked last for that year per the ranking rule. In FY2024, Tata reported 8 upheld complaints, JSW 1, SAIL 0 and Jindal 0; in FY2025, Tata rose to 18, JSW to 6, SAIL to 2 and Jindal to 4. The FY2025 picture therefore shows a sector-wide worsening in upheld cases, but Tata has the highest absolute number. Tata’s FY2025 BRSR confirms 18 standalone POSH complaints upheld and describes non-retaliation protections, escalation routes and SA8000-linked human rights expectations for vendors; it also states 100% of Tata Steel plants and offices in India are assessed on key human-rights issues including sexual harassment. Jindal Stainless’ FY2025 BRSR confirms 4 upheld complaints out of 5 reported POSH complaints, indicating a high substantiation ratio, and notes that three complaints were disposed of during the year while two were subsequently resolved. SAIL’s FY2024 BRSR shows 0 upheld complaints in FY2024 against 3 reported POSH complaints, after 3 upheld in FY2023 against 7 reported.

TATA_POSITIONING:
In FY2025, Tata ranks 4th with 18 POSH complaints upheld; SAIL leads with 2, so Tata is 800% higher than the best performer on raw count. Tata’s ranking did not improve across the comparable period: FY2023 is not comparable because upheld values were not reported, while Tata ranked 4th in both FY2024 and FY2025. The deterioration from 8 to 18 upheld cases is material because upheld cases indicate substantiated misconduct, not merely reporting volume. The management focus should therefore move beyond awareness and reporting access to prevention: site-level risk mapping, supervisor conduct training, IC root-cause reviews, contractor-interface controls, hotspot analysis across mines/steelworks/downstream units, and closure-quality assurance.

BENCHMARK: ethics-compliance

FY2022-23:
For FY2022-23, the apparent leaders on total POSH complaints are JSW Steel and Jindal Stainless, both reporting zero complaints in the standardised table, while Tata Steel is the laggard with 38 complaints and SAIL sits mid-pack with 8. However, the year is weak for benchmarking because upheld complaints are missing for all four companies, and Tata’s FY2022-23 figure is flagged as consolidated, including Indian and international operations, while the other company-years are standalone. The more defensible conclusion is that Tata had the highest reported case load, not necessarily the weakest system. Higher reporting may reflect greater scale, broader employee/worker coverage, more mature grievance routes and stronger speak-up culture. SAIL’s own FY2023 materiality discussion identifies human rights and ethics/code of conduct as material governance/social risks, indicating that POSH sits within a broader compliance and reputational-risk frame for large steel operators.

FY2023-24:
FY2023-24 gives a clearer standalone comparison. Jindal Stainless leads on total POSH complaints with 0 and also ties with SAIL for best performance on upheld complaints with 0. JSW follows with 1 total complaint and 1 upheld complaint. SAIL reported 3 total and 0 upheld, improving materially from its FY2022-23 reported level. Tata remains the laggard on both metrics, with 20 total complaints and 8 upheld in the standardised table. Tata’s own FY2023-24 BRSR shows a strong process architecture: written POSH complaints to the Internal Committee, confidential handling, investigation including evidence and witness review, non-victimisation safeguards, awareness and training, and whistle-blower protection. This indicates that Tata’s weaker raw ranking may partly reflect higher reporting maturity. Still, the upheld count suggests there were substantiated behavioural failures requiring prevention-oriented action, not just disclosure-system interpretation.

FY2024-25:
FY2024-25 is the primary benchmark year. SAIL is the clear leader on raw count, with 3 total POSH complaints and 2 upheld; Jindal is second with 5 total and 4 upheld; JSW is third with 7 total and 6 upheld; Tata is fourth with 43 total and 18 upheld. Tata’s FY2025 BRSR confirms a robust formal ecosystem: Speak Up and other reporting channels, Ethics Department investigation, escalation to the Chief Ethics Counsellor or Audit Committee Chairperson, Board/Audit Committee updates, non-retaliation safeguards, and reasonable assurance over standalone POSH indicators. The key management implication is that Tata’s issue is not absence of governance infrastructure; it is the high volume and substantiation of cases within that infrastructure. SAIL’s lower FY2025 count should be treated as the benchmark on outcome, while Tata should benchmark internally by site, workforce segment and complainant journey to distinguish incidence reduction from reporting suppression.
