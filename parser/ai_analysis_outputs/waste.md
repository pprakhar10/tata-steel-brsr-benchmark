FY2022-23:
Jindal Stainless was the strongest performer on absolute waste generation, with only 1.63 million MT total waste generated versus SAIL at 12.72 million MT, JSW at 14.41 million MT and Tata Steel at 22.09 million MT. However, Tata’s FY2023 value is consolidated and therefore structurally not comparable with the standalone peer figures. On recovery, Tata led with 20.15 million MT recovered, ahead of JSW and SAIL, showing strong circularity at scale, while SAIL led on low disposal with only 3,671 MT disposed. Jindal was the laggard on total disposal because it reported 766,299.7 MT disposed, largely under other disposal. The structural story is clear: Jindal’s smaller stainless/EAF footprint reduces absolute waste, SAIL’s integrated route generates large slag/dust streams but uses strong recovery practices, and Tata’s consolidated boundary inflates absolute values.

FY2023-24:
Jindal again led on absolute waste generation at 1.58 million MT, while Tata had the highest absolute waste at 16.83 million MT. Tata led total waste recovered at 18.88 million MT, followed by JSW at 15.08 million MT and SAIL at 14.37 million MT, indicating that Tata’s circularity engine remained strong even though absolute waste was high. The clearest laggard was Tata on disposal, with 307,577 MT disposed, mainly due to 303,496 MT landfilled, whereas SAIL reported the lowest disposal at 2,358 MT. Jindal was strongest on turnover-based waste intensity, while JSW led physical-output intensity at 0.7 MT/tcs. Tata’s FY2024 profile was therefore mixed: strong recovery leadership, but weak landfill/disposal performance. Tata’s disclosed practice of recycling/reusing waste or selling co-products to industries such as cement is strategically important, but the FY2024 landfill number shows execution or timing issues.

FY2024-25:
FY2025 is Tata’s strongest comparative year on recovery and a materially improved year on disposal. Tata led total waste recovered at 19.44 million MT and ranked 2nd on total waste disposed at 20,100 MT, after reducing disposal sharply from FY2024. Tata also ranked 2nd on physical-output intensity at 0.8 MT/tcs, behind SAIL at 0.75 MT/tcs. Jindal remained the absolute waste-generation and turnover-intensity leader, reflecting smaller scale and stainless/EAF circularity, while JSW led recycling and total disposal. SAIL’s profile was bifurcated: best physical-output intensity and very high other recovery, but weakest total disposal due to a 969,314 MT landfill figure linked to old stock. For Tata Steel management, the key message is that Tata’s recovery system is peer-leading, but further improvement is needed in category-specific regulated wastes, especially battery waste, e-waste, biomedical waste, plastic waste and hazardous waste, where Tata ranked 4th in FY2025.
