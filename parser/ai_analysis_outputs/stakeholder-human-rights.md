ABOUT:
This metric measures the share of direct sourcing from MSMEs or small producers and is aligned to BRSR Principle 8, which covers inclusive growth, equitable development and preferential procurement. It is an Essential Indicator because it reflects whether procurement spend supports smaller enterprises rather than only large raw-material suppliers. A higher value indicates better performance.

PERFORMANCE_AND_DRIVERS:
In FY2024-25, Jindal Stainless led at 11.04%, Tata Steel followed closely at 10%, JSW was at 5.58%, and SAIL fell sharply to 5.13%; the prior-year comparison shows SAIL was the leader in FY2023-24 at 29.28%, while Tata was second at 9% and Jindal was lowest at 5.38%. The sharp SAIL drop contrasts with its FY2024-25 supplier-development disclosures: 56 vendor development programmes for MSEs, 27 special programmes for SC/ST and women MSEs, and ₹2,586.52 crore MSE procurement representing 35.32% of eligible procurement, but the BRSR note clarifies that MSE figures are only for materials procured, so the standardized “directly sourced” denominator appears more restrictive.

Structurally, integrated BF-BOF steelmakers with captive mines and imported coking coal will naturally route large spend through captive/inter-company, large mining, logistics, bulk commodity and technology suppliers. This depresses MSME share even where vendor development is active. Jindal Stainless’ FY2024-25 improvement is consistent with a stainless-steel procurement model that has more distributed supplier categories around scrap, revert, ferro alloys, service vendors and downstream ecosystem support, while Tata’s 9% to 10% improvement suggests incremental progress without a step-change in sourcing architecture.

TATA_POSITIONING:
In FY2024-25, Tata ranked 2nd at 10%, trailing Jindal Stainless by 1.04 percentage points, or 9.4% below the leader on a relative basis. Tata’s rank held at 2nd in FY2023 and FY2024 and remained 2nd in FY2025, but the FY2023-to-FY2024 trend is not like-for-like because FY2022-23 is consolidated. The FY2024-to-FY2025 movement is directionally positive: Tata improved from 9% to 10%, while SAIL collapsed from 29.28% to 5.13%, JSW declined from 6.9% to 5.58%, and Jindal accelerated from 5.38% to 11.04%.

ABOUT:
This metric measures the percentage of inputs sourced through sustainable sourcing procedures and is a BRSR Principle 2 Essential Indicator. It reflects whether procurement is governed by defined supplier codes, ESG criteria, responsible sourcing policies and assurance mechanisms. A higher value indicates better performance.

PERFORMANCE_AND_DRIVERS:
Tata, JSW and SAIL all reported 100% in FY2023 and FY2024, while Jindal Stainless reported 90% in both years. In FY2025, Tata, JSW and Jindal reported 100%, while SAIL dropped to 0% in the standardized dataset. Tata’s 100% is supported by supplier sign-off to the Business Associate Code of Conduct and a Responsible Supply Chain Policy covering applicable laws, anti-bribery, health and safety, human rights, environmental protection, asset safeguarding and conflict-of-interest avoidance; Tata further disclosed that 100% of suppliers in India commit to sustainable sourcing.

The metric is sensitive to definition. For integrated BF-BOF companies, “sustainable sourcing” can be reported as policy coverage across supplier onboarding rather than physical low-carbon or certified-green inputs. This explains why companies with very different steel routes and raw-material footprints can all report 100%. SAIL’s FY2025 narrative states that it incorporates ESG principles into sourcing, uses IMS-certified captive mines and imports coal from jurisdictions with stringent ESG standards, but the standardized value is 0%, indicating a possible conversion, response-field or quantification issue rather than a simple absence of procedures.

TATA_POSITIONING:
In FY2024-25, Tata ranked joint 1st at 100%, tied with JSW and Jindal Stainless; SAIL was the only laggard at 0%. Tata’s rank has effectively held steady across all three years, although the FY2023 basis is consolidated. The main management implication is that Tata is already at ceiling level on policy-coverage reporting; future differentiation will need to come from deeper proof points such as high-risk category coverage, supplier maturity bands, third-party audits, ResponsibleSteel-equivalent certifications, Scope 3 supplier decarbonisation engagement and contract-level ESG incentives.

ABOUT:

It indicates the extent to which plant-level procurement supports surrounding industrial ecosystems, service providers and community-linked suppliers. A higher value indicates better performance.

In FY2024-25, JSW led at 67.22%, followed by Tata at 63%, Jindal at 41% and SAIL at 31.36%. The structural driver is plant geography and procurement perimeter. Tata’s Jamshedpur/Kalinganagar ecosystem, local services, maintenance, stores and downstream industrial clusters support a high local sourcing share, but bulk raw materials such as coking coal, iron ore, limestone and ferro alloys remain structurally non-local or captive. JSW’s 65% and 67.22% values indicate either strong local procurement design or a standardized conversion that captures a large district-neighbourhood spend base. Jindal’s 41–42% local share is healthy for stainless operations but constrained by alloying metals and scrap/ferroalloy supply chains. Tata’s own FY2024-to-FY2025 movement was marginally negative, from 64% to 63%, but the level remains strong for an integrated steelmaker with large cross-regional raw-material and logistics flows.

FY2022-23:
The FY2022-23 benchmark is led by Tata on breadth of supply-chain due diligence and local sourcing, while SAIL leads only on MSME/small-producer sourcing. Tata ranked 1st on value-chain environmental assessment at 78%, 1st on sustainable sourcing at 100% tied with JSW and SAIL, and 1st on local sourcing at 41%; SAIL’s 32.71% MSME sourcing narrowly beat Tata’s 32%. Jindal Stainless was weaker on sustainable sourcing at 90% and did not report value-chain environmental assessment, while JSW had low MSME sourcing at 5%, zero environmental assessment and very low local sourcing at 2.8%. Tata’s FY2022-23 data, however, is consolidated and includes Indian and international operations, so the apparent leadership should not be read as purely India-standalone outperformance. Overall, Tata’s position reflects a more mature responsible sourcing architecture, while SAIL’s MSME result reflects public-sector procurement obligations and vendor-development orientation.

FY2023-24:
In FY2023-24, Tata and SAIL split leadership by indicator, but Tata had the more balanced profile. Tata ranked 1st for value-chain environmental assessment at 74% and tied 1st for sustainable sourcing at 100%, while SAIL led MSME/small-producer sourcing at 29.28% and tied 1st for sustainable sourcing at 100%. JSW led local sourcing at 65%, just ahead of Tata’s 64%, but it remained weak on value-chain environmental assessment at 0%. Jindal Stainless remained behind on sustainable sourcing at 90%, but its 16% value-chain environmental assessment showed an emerging supplier ESG programme. Tata’s disclosures show RSCP-based supplier maturity assessment and a 4-step Sustainable Procurement Framework planned for FY2025 buying decisions, giving it a stronger assurance pathway than peers with high headline values but thinner assessment coverage.

FY2024-25:
In FY2024-25, Jindal Stainless became the strongest thematic mover because it ranked 1st on MSME/small-producer sourcing at 11.04%, 1st on value-chain environmental assessment at 100%, and tied 1st on sustainable sourcing at 100%; its weaker point was local sourcing at 41%, below Tata and JSW. Tata remained the most balanced large integrated steel peer: 2nd on MSME sourcing at 10%, 2nd on environmental assessment at 80%, tied 1st on sustainable sourcing at 100%, and 2nd on local sourcing at 63%. JSW led local sourcing at 67.22% and tied for sustainable sourcing, but was weaker on MSME and environmental assessment. SAIL was the laggard overall in FY2025, ranking last on MSME, environmental assessment, sustainable sourcing and local sourcing, despite strong MSE vendor-development disclosures and certified captive-mine/sourcing systems. For Tata management, the FY2025 priority is not policy existence but conversion of supplier programmes into 100% value-based environmental assessment and stronger MSME participation without diluting BF-BOF raw-material security.
