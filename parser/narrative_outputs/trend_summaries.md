TREND_SUMMARY: tata-steel
Tata Steel’s three-year ESG trajectory is best read with caution because FY2022-23 was reported on a consolidated basis, while FY2023-24 and FY2024-25 are standalone. The rank distribution shows modest improvement in peer position over time, with Rank 1 indicators rising from 22 in FY2022-23 to 27 in FY2024-25, and Rank 4 indicators reducing from 42 to 34. However, FY2024-25 is a mixed year: 34 indicators improved, but 40 worsened, suggesting that Tata’s current ESG profile remains strong in systems, scale and governance, but uneven in measured outcomes.
The clearest strengths are training, waste recovery, sustainability investment, responsible sourcing and market-channel transparency. Tata maintained 100% training coverage across employees and workers, led FY2024-25 waste recovery at 19.44 million MT, reduced waste disposal sharply, and ranked strongly on sustainability capex and R&D alignment. Its supplier ESG architecture also remains comparatively mature, with 80% value-chain environmental assessment and 100% sustainable sourcing, supported by the Responsible Supply Chain Policy and sustainable procurement framework.
The main weaknesses are environmental intensity and safety outcomes. Tata ranked weakly on GHG emissions, energy intensity and water intensity in FY2024-25, with 3.2 tCO2e/tcs emissions intensity and 28.3 GJ/tcs energy intensity, both behind key peers. Its Net Zero ambition is therefore strategically credible only if accelerated through blast-furnace decarbonisation, renewable power, scrap/EAF transition and Scope 3 management. Safety also remains a priority: although programmes such as ISO 45001-based EHSMS, fatality risk controls, contractor audits and EnsafeNxt show strong governance, Tata still ranked last on key LTIFR/TRIFR indicators. Overall, Tata’s ESG position is institutionally mature but performance-conversion gaps remain material.
TREND_SUMMARY: jsw-steel
JSW Steel’s ESG trajectory shows relative consistency in leadership positions, but a weakening movement profile in FY2024-25. Its Rank 1 indicators stayed broadly stable, moving from 34 in FY2022-23 to 34 in FY2023-24 and 35 in FY2024-25, while Rank 4 indicators improved from 15 to 10 over the period. However, the trend data shows a clear reversal in FY2024-25: after 36 indicators improved in FY2023-24, only 22 improved in FY2024-25, while 46 worsened. This suggests that JSW remains a strong peer benchmark in selected areas, but the latest year shows loss of momentum across several indicators.
JSW’s strongest position is in renewable energy scaling, water efficiency and product disclosure. It led renewable energy and renewable electricity in FY2024-25, reaching 3.23 million GJ and 3.06 million GJ respectively, and retained the best operational water intensity at 2.369 kL/tcs while reporting zero discharge. It also remained strong on product information, with 100% coverage for environmental/social product information and recycling/safe-disposal information. Training coverage stayed at 100% across all four reported categories, keeping JSW aligned with Tata and Jindal on disclosure-level capability development.
The weaker areas are air emissions, safety movement and related-party concentration. JSW was the FY2024-25 laggard on SOx, NOx and PM, indicating that its environmental-control outcomes are not matching its renewable-energy progress. Health and safety also weakened on worker TRIFR and affected workers, although fatalities remained comparatively controlled. Governance indicators show high related-party concentration in investments, loans/advances and sales, requiring strong internal oversight even where not inherently problematic. Overall, JSW remains a broad-scale, financially strong ESG competitor, but its FY2024-25 profile suggests plateauing in several operational ESG outcomes despite continued renewable and disclosure strengths.
TREND_SUMMARY: sail
SAIL’s three-year ESG trajectory is uneven, with a strong improvement phase in FY2023-24 followed by partial slippage in FY2024-25. Rank 1 indicators increased from 28 in FY2022-23 to 38 in FY2023-24, before easing to 34 in FY2024-25. Rank 4 indicators reduced from 31 to 24 in FY2023-24, but increased again to 28 in FY2024-25. The movement data tells a similar story: 36 indicators improved in FY2023-24, but FY2024-25 saw 47 worsened indicators against 27 improved. This indicates that SAIL has made meaningful progress, but the improvements are not yet stable across the full ESG scorecard.
SAIL’s clearest strengths are selected air-emission outcomes, welfare expenditure, related-party governance and some process-efficiency initiatives. In FY2024-25, it led SOx and NOx among peers and also led employee wellbeing expenditure at 1.44% of revenue. Its public-sector structure gives it the cleanest related-party profile on investments, loans/advances and purchases. It also improved energy performance materially, supported by disclosed initiatives such as coke dry quenching, top-pressure recovery turbines, coal dust injection, waste heat recovery, walking beam furnaces, renewable capacity plans and green hydrogen pilots. Its specific PM emission reduction target is therefore well aligned with a visible operational priority.
However, SAIL remains structurally challenged by legacy integrated BF-BOF assets, high absolute energy and water footprint, weak training coverage for employees and missing worker skill-upgradation disclosure. In FY2024-25, it became the laggard on stakeholder and supply-chain indicators, including MSME sourcing, value-chain environmental assessment, sustainable sourcing and local sourcing. Product information also dropped to 0% in the standardised table. Overall, SAIL shows credible improvement in selected heavy-industry controls, but still lacks the disclosure depth, supplier ESG coverage and workforce capability consistency seen in stronger private-sector peers.
TREND_SUMMARY: jindal-stainless
Jindal Stainless shows the strongest overall multi-year ESG improvement among the four companies. Its Rank 1 indicators rose sharply from 37 in FY2022-23 to 53 in FY2023-24 and 61 in FY2024-25, while Rank 4 indicators fell from 37 to 17 and then to only 8. Unlike the other peers, its FY2024-25 movement profile remains positive, with 43 indicators improving, 25 worsening and 29 stable. This makes Jindal the clearest upward mover in the peer set, although its smaller stainless-steel scale and different process route must be considered when comparing it with integrated carbon-steel producers.
The strongest trend is environmental performance. Jindal led FY2024-25 GHG and Scope 3 indicators, with 1.85 tCO2e/tcs physical emissions intensity, supported by scrap-based EAF production, renewable-energy sourcing, biofuel replacement, waste-heat recovery, hot charging, liquid chrome use, VFD installations and auxiliary power reduction. It also led energy intensity at 16.36 GJ/tcs and remained strong on absolute water withdrawal and consumption. Its stated targets on 50% carbon-emission-intensity reduction and Zero-Waste-to-Landfill certification align well with its existing process advantages and circularity positioning.
Jindal also improved sharply in safety, training, stakeholder assessment and product responsibility. FY2024-25 disclosures show zero LTIFR, zero fatalities and zero affected employees/workers, while training recovered to 100% across all four categories. Value-chain environmental assessment reached 100%, sustainable sourcing reached 100%, and product information coverage also reached 100%. The main weaknesses are female employee representation, trading-house dependence and the need to sustain improvements as scale grows. Overall, Jindal has moved from a disclosure and scale-limited player to the strongest FY2024-25 ESG benchmark across several operational themes, especially carbon, energy, safety and supplier ESG.
