COMPANY_NARRATIVE: tata-steel / FY2023

Tata Steel’s FY2023 ESG position is difficult to benchmark cleanly because the filing was consolidated, including international operations, while the peer set is otherwise standalone. That boundary effect explains many of its weak environmental ranks: Tata ranked fourth on 42 of 98 indicators, with high absolute Scope 1 emissions, Scope 3 emissions, water withdrawal, water discharge and waste generation inflated by global operations. Even so, the year shows real strengths. Tata led on net worth, energy intensity per rupee of turnover, renewable “other sources” energy, rehabilitation support for affected employees and workers, sustainability capex, and value-chain environmental assessment. Its recovery of 20.15 million MT of waste was the best in the set, indicating a circularity engine operating at large scale. Socially, Tata led employee gender representation and reported 100% training coverage across employee and worker health, safety and skills, but it lagged on safety frequency rates and affected-person counts. POSH complaints were highest, but this should be read as a combination of scale, reporting maturity and real conduct risk rather than a simple governance failure. Strategically, FY2023 shows Tata as the most mature system player, with strong balance sheet, sourcing architecture and investment orientation, but also as the most exposed to BF-BOF carbon intensity, multinational boundary complexity and high-consequence safety risk. The year should be treated as a directional baseline, not a like-for-like peer ranking.

COMPANY_NARRATIVE: jsw-steel / FY2023

JSW Steel was the strongest broad ESG performer in FY2023 on the standardised peer table, with 34 rank-one and 29 rank-two outcomes across 98 indicators and only 15 rank-four outcomes. Its position was especially strong on product-responsibility disclosures, renewable energy scale, selected waste categories, employee fatality performance and turnover. JSW led the peer set on turnover and ranked ahead of Tata, SAIL and Jindal on renewable energy consumption, with 1.43 million GJ reported. It also reported 100% coverage for product environmental/social information and recycling or safe-disposal information, placing it with SAIL at the top of the consumer-responsibility theme. Environmentally, JSW’s profile was mixed rather than uniformly leading. It was better than Tata and SAIL on energy intensity and total energy, but Jindal’s stainless/EAF structure was stronger on absolute emissions, water and air pollutants. JSW’s Scope 3 leadership should be treated cautiously because it may reflect narrower coverage rather than structurally superior value-chain performance. Socially, JSW reported 100% training coverage and no employee fatalities, but was weaker on wellbeing spend and POSH upheld complaints. Governance and value-chain indicators were uneven, with weak MSME sourcing, limited environmental assessment of value-chain partners and missing RPT metrics. Overall, FY2023 shows JSW as the strongest quantitative all-rounder in the table, driven by scale, disclosure coverage and operational efficiency, but not yet the environmental intensity benchmark.

COMPANY_NARRATIVE: sail / FY2023

SAIL’s FY2023 ESG profile reflected the strengths and constraints of a large, public-sector, integrated steelmaker. It ranked first on 28 indicators but fourth on 31, producing a mid-pack overall position. Environmentally, SAIL’s legacy BF-BOF and captive/process-energy footprint weighed heavily: it was the laggard on physical GHG intensity at 2.8 tCO2e/tcs, performed weakly on per-rupee Scope 1 and 2 intensity, and ranked last on energy physical intensity because the standardised value was missing. It also lacked comparable SOx, NOx and PM disclosures in the table, weakening its air-emissions benchmark. At the same time, SAIL showed strengths in construction and demolition waste, e-waste, low waste disposal and MSME sourcing. Socially, SAIL was comparatively strong on worker and employee LTIFR and was the only company with female KMP representation in FY2023, but it lagged on worker fatalities and training breadth outside worker H&S. Its 100% sustainable sourcing and leading MSME sourcing reflected public-sector procurement orientation and vendor-development obligations. Financially, SAIL remained a major scale player, with turnover above ₹104,000 crore, but below Tata and JSW. The strategic picture is of a large domestic steel institution with strong procurement reach and some safety strengths, but constrained by older integrated assets, energy intensity, uneven disclosure completeness and carbon-heavy process architecture.

COMPANY_NARRATIVE: jindal-stainless / FY2023

Jindal Stainless presented the sharpest ESG contrast in FY2023: it had the highest number of rank-one outcomes at 37, but also 37 rank-four outcomes. The company’s environmental position was structurally strong because of its smaller stainless-steel, scrap-linked and EAF-oriented footprint. It led the GHG and Scope 3 theme on Scope 1, Scope 2 and physical-output intensity, reporting 2.58 MtCO2e Scope 1, 0.74 MtCO2e Scope 2 and 2.1 tCO2e/tcs. It was also the clear air-emissions leader and the best absolute water and energy performer, with low total withdrawal, consumption and energy intensity. Jindal’s BRSR confirms a standalone boundary and a stainless-focused product base, with exports contributing about 15.49% of turnover, reinforcing its niche, higher-export structural profile. The weaknesses were mostly disclosure, scale and people-system related. It was last on net worth and turnover due to its smaller business model, last on employee gender diversity, weak on product-information coverage and poor on worker LTIFR. Waste was also mixed: lowest generation but highest total disposal. Governance indicators were thin, with very low sustainability capex and R&D alignment. Overall, FY2023 shows Jindal as the environmental intensity benchmark among the four, but with a less mature social, governance and product-responsibility disclosure platform.

COMPANY_NARRATIVE: tata-steel / FY2024

Tata Steel’s FY2024 standalone comparison gives a clearer and tougher view than FY2023. It ranked first on 26 indicators but fourth on 38, showing a strong strategic platform but weak relative outcomes in several operating ESG metrics. Tata was the financial leader, with the highest turnover and net worth, and remained the best workforce-diversity performer by female employee share and female wage share. It also sustained 100% training coverage and led value-chain environmental assessment at 74%, supported by a Responsible Supply Chain Policy covering health and safety, fair business practices, environmental protection and human rights. Its R&D and capex disclosures were strategically strong: 100% of R&D spend was aligned to NGRBC principles, and sustainability capex included CO2, SOx, NOx, dust reduction, water conservation, effluent treatment, waste utilisation, safety and employee welfare. The weaknesses were material. Tata ranked last on Scope 1, Scope 2, Scope 3 and physical GHG intensity, with 3.1 tCO2e/tcs, and also lagged on water withdrawal, water consumption, waste disposal, POSH complaints and all core safety frequency indicators. Circularity remained a strength, including 4.3 million tonnes of scrap recycled in FY2023-24, but landfill and regulated-waste outcomes weakened the environmental story. FY2024 therefore shows Tata as the most institutionally mature player, but not the operational ESG benchmark.

COMPANY_NARRATIVE: jsw-steel / FY2024

JSW Steel delivered the strongest overall FY2024 peer-table performance after Jindal, with 34 rank-one and 32 rank-two indicators and only 9 rank-four indicators. Its ESG profile was balanced and commercially relevant. Environmentally, JSW led renewable energy and renewable electricity, with 1.98 million GJ renewable energy, and was the operational water-intensity leader at 2.39 kL/tcs while also reporting zero total water discharge. It also performed well on physical waste intensity and product-responsibility disclosures, reporting 100% coverage for both environmental/social product information and recycling or safe-disposal information. However, it was not the carbon benchmark: Jindal remained superior on GHG intensity, Scope 1, Scope 2 and air pollutants, while JSW ranked poorly on NOx. Socially, JSW was one of the two training leaders, with 100% coverage across employee and worker H&S and skills. It led worker LTIFR, reported zero employee fatalities and maintained mid-pack diversity. Its main social weakness was very low wellbeing expenditure. Governance was more mixed: JSW tied Tata on 100% R&D alignment but showed high related-party concentration in investments, loans/advances and sales. Strategically, JSW’s FY2024 position reflects a high-scale private-sector steelmaker with strong renewable procurement, water efficiency, product disclosure and training systems, but with continuing exposure to integrated steelmaking emissions, related-party flows and selected air-emission weaknesses.

COMPANY_NARRATIVE: sail / FY2024

SAIL improved its FY2024 ESG benchmark position materially, recording 38 rank-one outcomes, 20 rank-two outcomes and 24 rank-four outcomes. Its standalone boundary included five integrated steel plants, three special steel plants, multiple units and mines, making it a broad domestic operating footprint rather than a narrow corporate entity. Environmentally, SAIL remained constrained by integrated steelmaking and legacy asset intensity. It ranked last on total energy, physical energy intensity, energy intensity per rupee and employee fatalities, and remained weaker than Jindal and JSW on GHG operational metrics. However, it improved its air-emissions comparability and ranked second on SOx and NOx. It also had the lowest total waste disposal and led several waste categories. Socially, SAIL’s profile was split: it led employee LTIFR and POSH upheld complaints, and had the strongest wellbeing expenditure intensity at 1.46% of revenue, but lagged on training breadth and had weaker Board diversity. Governance was a relative strength. SAIL was the cleanest RPT concentration benchmark across investments, loans/advances, purchases and sales, reflecting its PSU structure and more internalised operating model. Strategically, FY2024 shows SAIL as improving and governance-stable, with strong welfare and procurement characteristics, but still materially exposed to energy intensity, carbon intensity and training-system gaps.

COMPANY_NARRATIVE: jindal-stainless / FY2024

Jindal Stainless was the standout ESG performer in FY2024, with 53 rank-one outcomes out of 98 and only 17 rank-four outcomes. Its environmental leadership was broad and structural. It ranked first on Scope 1, Scope 2, Scope 3, physical GHG intensity and Scope 1+2 turnover intensity, reporting 2.15 tCO2e/tcs and making its Scope 3 disclosure more comparable than in FY2023. It also led on absolute energy, physical energy intensity, water withdrawal, water consumption, zero discharge, air pollutants and absolute waste generation. The BRSR confirms a standalone boundary, reasonable assurance by SGS India and a stainless-focused business with exports of about 17.07% of turnover, reinforcing that the company’s profile is both structurally different and increasingly disclosure-mature. The year was not flawless. Jindal’s training coverage broke sharply across employee and worker H&S and skills, suggesting either reporting-boundary/classification issues or programme-capture weakness. It also lagged on female employee share, wage share, MSME sourcing and some trading-house/procurement indicators. Governance improved significantly through leading sustainability capex at 23.42%, but related-party loans and procurement-route complexity remained watchpoints. Overall, FY2024 shows Jindal as the environmental benchmark, especially for Tata’s decarbonisation and resource-efficiency comparison, while highlighting that Board diversity and environmental intensity do not yet translate into workforce inclusion or training consistency.

COMPANY_NARRATIVE: tata-steel / FY2025

Tata Steel’s FY2025 ESG position was strategically stronger than its operational ranking suggests. It ranked first on 27 indicators and second on 15, but still had 34 rank-four outcomes. Tata remained the scale leader on turnover and the strongest private-sector player on employee gender diversity, while improving wellbeing spend and sustaining 100% training coverage across a very large employee and worker base. Its sustainability investment signal was the strongest in the peer set: 37% sustainability capex and 100% R&D alignment. Tata’s own materiality disclosure acknowledges that blast furnace production is emission-intensive and ties long-term competitiveness to Net Zero 2045, decarbonising BF operations and building a low-emission EAF plant in Ludhiana. Environmentally, the gap remains significant. Tata ranked last on Scope 1, Scope 2, Scope 3 and physical GHG intensity, and its 3.2 tCO2e/tcs was far above Jindal’s 1.85. It also lagged on physical energy intensity, water consumption and several regulated-waste categories. Offsetting this, Tata led waste recovery and materially reduced disposal. Socially, safety remained the biggest weakness: despite ISO 45001-based systems and supplier safety controls, Tata ranked fourth on LTIFR, TRIFR and affected-person metrics. Its vendor assessment model, VCAP programme and reassessment process show governance depth, but FY2025’s message is clear: Tata has the strongest transition architecture, but peers are ahead on disclosed operating outcomes.

COMPANY_NARRATIVE: jsw-steel / FY2025

JSW Steel remained a strong ESG all-rounder in FY2025, with 35 rank-one indicators, 24 rank-two indicators and only 10 rank-four outcomes. Its main environmental strength was renewable energy scale: it led both renewable energy and renewable electricity, reaching 3.23 million GJ and 3.06 million GJ respectively. It also remained the best operational water-intensity performer at 2.369 kL/tcs and reported zero discharge, making it the most relevant water-efficiency benchmark for Tata. Product responsibility was another strong area, with 100% coverage for environmental/social product information and recycling or safe-disposal information. Weaknesses were concentrated in air emissions, governance concentration and wellbeing. JSW ranked last on SOx, NOx and particulate matter in FY2025, indicating that renewable power progress had not translated into leadership on local air pollutants. It was also weak on wellbeing expenditure and related-party investment concentration. Socially, JSW sustained 100% training coverage and remained competitive on fatalities, but worker TRIFR and affected-worker outcomes worsened relative to the leading peer. Financially, it stayed close to Tata on turnover and led net worth where Tata’s standardised value was missing. Overall, JSW’s FY2025 profile is that of a high-scale, efficiency-oriented competitor with strong renewable procurement, water performance and product disclosure, but with meaningful gaps in air emissions, welfare intensity and RPT governance exposure.

COMPANY_NARRATIVE: sail / FY2025

SAIL’s FY2025 ESG profile was mixed, with 34 rank-one outcomes but 28 rank-four outcomes. Its standalone reporting boundary remained broad, covering integrated steel plants, special steel plants, units and captive mines, and it obtained reasonable assurance for the BRSR. Environmentally, SAIL showed notable improvement in total energy and physical energy intensity, supported by efficiency and decarbonisation measures identified in the benchmark analysis, including CDQ, TRT, CDI, waste heat recovery, renewable capacity plans and green hydrogen pilots. It also led SOx and NOx emissions in FY2025, a major improvement in local air-emissions positioning. However, it became the absolute water-withdrawal and discharge laggard, remained weak on Scope 2 and physical GHG intensity, and had the weakest total waste disposal due to old-stock landfill. Social performance was split. SAIL led wellbeing expenditure at 1.44% of revenue and female wage share, but reported zero female Board representation and continued to have worker fatalities. Training remained a persistent weakness: 100% worker H&S coverage was not matched by employee H&S, employee skills or worker skill-upgradation reporting. Governance remained comparatively clean on RPT concentration because of its PSU structure, but stakeholder and product-responsibility indicators deteriorated, especially sustainable sourcing, value-chain assessment and product-information coverage. FY2025 positions SAIL as improving operationally in energy and air, but lagging in water, training, inclusion governance and supplier ESG maturity.

COMPANY_NARRATIVE: jindal-stainless / FY2025

Jindal Stainless was the clear FY2025 ESG benchmark, with 61 rank-one outcomes and only 8 rank-four outcomes. The performance was led by structural environmental advantage and stronger disclosure maturity. It ranked first on Scope 1, Scope 2, Scope 3, physical GHG intensity and Scope 1+2 turnover intensity, with 1.85 tCO2e/tcs, reflecting stainless-steel scrap/EAF economics, renewable sourcing, biofuel replacement, waste-heat recovery, hot charging and process-efficiency initiatives. It also led absolute energy, non-renewable energy, physical energy intensity, absolute water withdrawal and consumption, turnover water intensity, particulate matter and waste-generation intensity. The FY2025 BRSR was prepared on a standalone basis and obtained reasonable assurance from SGS India, including BRSR Core, non-core indicators and GHG Scope 1, 2 and 3 disclosures. Socially, Jindal was the safety leader by a wide margin, ranking first on LTIFR, TRIFR, fatalities and affected-person indicators, and recovered to 100% training coverage across all four training metrics. Governance and stakeholder performance also improved: it led value-chain environmental assessment at 100%, tied on sustainable sourcing and had the best Board diversity at 27.28%. The weaknesses were narrower but important: female employee share and wage share remained last, total waste recovered was low due to scale and stainless mix, and trading-house dependence remained high. Overall, FY2025 shows Jindal as the operational ESG leader, though not the scale, workforce-diversity or channel-transparency benchmark.
