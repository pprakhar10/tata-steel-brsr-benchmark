**FY2024-25**

Tata’s FY2025 BRSR confirms a robust formal ecosystem: Speak Up and other reporting channels, Ethics Department investigation, escalation to the Chief Ethics Counsellor or Audit Committee Chairperson, Board/Audit Committee updates, non-retaliation safeguards, and reasonable assurance over standalone POSH indicators. The key management implication is that Tata’s issue is not absence of governance infrastructure; it is the high volume and substantiation of cases within that infrastructure. SAIL’s lower FY2025 count should be treated as the benchmark on outcome, while Tata should benchmark internally by site, workforce segment and complainant journey to distinguish incidence reduction from reporting suppression.

Jindal’s low FY2023 base also reflects the early stage of its later FY2024/FY2025 sustainability investment ramp.

**FY2023-24**
FY2023-24 is the first meaningful year for RPT benchmarking. SAIL is the governance concentration leader on investments, loans/advances, purchases, and sales, with 81.66% related-party investments, 1.15% loans/advances, 6.66% purchases, and just 0.06% sales. Tata is mixed: strong on sales at 12% but weak on purchases at 40% and high on related-party investments at 96%. Jindal sits between SAIL and Tata/JSW on purchases at 7% and investments at 89%, but is high on loans/advances at 100%. JSW shows high related-party concentration in investments, loans/advances, and sales. On sustainability capex, Jindal leads at 23.42%, ahead of Tata at 18%, while Tata and JSW tie at 100% R&D. The structural story is clear: SAIL’s standalone PSU operating structure reduces RPT intensity, while Tata/JSW/Jindal group ecosystems create more related-party flows that require stronger governance controls rather than simple ratio-based judgement.

**FY2024-25**
FY2024-25 shows Tata’s strongest overall position in the combined theme, but not across all RPT metrics. Tata leads sales-to-related-parties at 13.04%, leads sustainability capex at 37%, and ties for rank 1 on R&D at 100%. However, Tata is still behind SAIL and Jindal on related-party investments, behind SAIL on loans/advances, and last on related-party purchases at 37.78%. SAIL remains the cleanest RPT concentration benchmark on investments, loans/advances, and purchases, reflecting its standalone public-sector structure and internalised plant/mine/unit footprint. Jindal is the strongest stainless peer on low purchase concentration and improving sustainability R&D, rising to 51.98%, supported by product-longevity, corrosion-resistance, import-substitution, and renewable/WHRB/ZLD-linked projects. JSW remains numerically high on RPT concentration but ties Tata on R&D. For Tata senior management, the headline is: FY2025 sustainability investment leadership is strong, sales-side RPT exposure is best-in-peer, but purchase-side and investment-side related-party concentration remain the key governance watchpoints.

### Financial Profile

**FY2022-23**
The overall FY2022-23 financial-profile leader is Tata Steel if balance-sheet strength is weighted alongside turnover and exports: Tata led net worth at ₹103,082.0 crore and was nearly tied with JSW on turnover after normalising raw INR-style entries into INR crore. JSW was the turnover leader at ₹130,039.0 crore, narrowly ahead of Tata’s ~₹129,006.6 crore, while Jindal Stainless was the clear export-intensity leader at 15.49%. The main structural difference is portfolio and boundary: Tata’s FY2022-23 data is flagged consolidated, including international operations, while JSW, SAIL, and Jindal are standalone. SAIL’s scale remained material at ~₹104,447.4 crore turnover but lower than Tata/JSW, and its annual report highlights a difficult global steel cycle with energy crisis, inflation, supply-chain disruption, rate hikes, and volatile steel prices. Jindal Stainless was the laggard on absolute turnover and net worth because it is a stainless-focused player, not a broad BF-BOF integrated carbon-steel major, although it led export contribution.

**FY2023-24**
Tata Steel was the strongest overall FY2023-24 performer, ranking 1st on turnover and net worth, with ~₹142,913.3 crore turnover and ₹134,137.5 crore net worth. JSW remained the closest absolute-scale peer at ₹133,609.0 crore turnover and ₹67,903.0 crore net worth, but the gap to Tata was wider on capital base than on revenue. SAIL was stable on turnover at ~₹104,545.1 crore and improved net worth to ₹54,130.5 crore, but remained behind Tata and JSW. Jindal Stainless continued to be the export leader at 17.07% and improved turnover to ~₹38,356.0 crore, demonstrating strong stainless demand and international market reach, but it remained the smallest by absolute financial scale. Tata’s FY2023-24 BRSR also explains a methodology issue: prior-year figures were restated following mergers and revised reporting boundaries, so the apparent improvement should be read with the restated standalone basis in mind.

**FY2024-25**
FY2024-25 is mixed but still favourable for Tata on operating scale. Tata ranked 1st on turnover at ₹132,516.7 crore, ahead of JSW at ₹125,678.0 crore and SAIL at ₹101,716.0 crore, while Jindal Stainless stayed much smaller at ~₹40,816.9 crore. Tata’s BRSR confirms standalone turnover of ₹132,516.66 crore, with steel products contributing 93%, and notes that consolidated turnover was much larger at ₹218,542.51 crore due to India, Europe, Thailand, and other entities. On net worth, JSW is the reported leader because Tata’s FY2024-25 value is missing in the standardised table; this should be treated as a data gap, not proof of weaker financial position. On exports, Jindal Stainless remained the clear leader at 11.69%, supported by stainless-steel global applications and 49 international markets, while Tata improved to 2nd because it held exports stable at 6% and JSW fell to 5.5%.

### Stakeholder Engagement & Human Rights

**FY2022-23**
The FY2022-23 benchmark is led by Tata on breadth of supply-chain due diligence and local sourcing, while SAIL leads only on MSME/small-producer sourcing. Tata ranked 1st on value-chain environmental assessment at 78%, 1st on sustainable sourcing at 100% tied with JSW and SAIL, and 1st on local sourcing at 41%; SAIL’s 32.71% MSME sourcing narrowly beat Tata’s 32%. Jindal Stainless was weaker on sustainable sourcing at 90% and did not report value-chain environmental assessment, while JSW had low MSME sourcing at 5%, zero environmental assessment and very low local sourcing at 2.8%. Tata’s FY2022-23 data, however, is consolidated and includes Indian and international operations, so the apparent leadership should not be read as purely India-standalone outperformance. Overall, Tata’s position reflects a more mature responsible sourcing architecture, while SAIL’s MSME result reflects public-sector procurement obligations and vendor-development orientation.

**FY2023-24**
In FY2023-24, Tata and SAIL split leadership by indicator, but Tata had the more balanced profile. Tata ranked 1st for value-chain environmental assessment at 74% and tied 1st for sustainable sourcing at 100%, while SAIL led MSME/small-producer sourcing at 29.28% and tied 1st for sustainable sourcing at 100%. JSW led local sourcing at 65%, just ahead of Tata’s 64%, but it remained weak on value-chain environmental assessment at 0%. Jindal Stainless remained behind on sustainable sourcing at 90%, but its 16% value-chain environmental assessment showed an emerging supplier ESG programme. Tata’s disclosures show RSCP-based supplier maturity assessment and a 4-step Sustainable Procurement Framework planned for FY2025 buying decisions, giving it a stronger assurance pathway than peers with high headline values but thinner assessment coverage.

**FY2024-25**
In FY2024-25, Jindal Stainless became the strongest thematic mover because it ranked 1st on MSME/small-producer sourcing at 11.04%, 1st on value-chain environmental assessment at 100%, and tied 1st on sustainable sourcing at 100%; its weaker point was local sourcing at 41%, below Tata and JSW. Tata remained the most balanced large integrated steel peer: 2nd on MSME sourcing at 10%, 2nd on environmental assessment at 80%, tied 1st on sustainable sourcing at 100%, and 2nd on local sourcing at 63%. JSW led local sourcing at 67.22% and tied for sustainable sourcing, but was weaker on MSME and environmental assessment. SAIL was the laggard overall in FY2025, ranking last on MSME, environmental assessment, sustainable sourcing and local sourcing, despite strong MSE vendor-development disclosures and certified captive-mine/sourcing systems. For Tata management, the FY2025 priority is not policy existence but conversion of supplier programmes into 100% value-based environmental assessment and stronger MSME participation without diluting BF-BOF raw-material security.

### CSR, Products & Consumer Responsibility

**FY2022-23**
FY2022-23 leadership is split. On product-information coverage, JSW and SAIL are the clear leaders, each reporting 100% for both environmental/social product information and recycling/safe-disposal information. Tata Steel is mid-pack at 50% and 19%, while Jindal Stainless is the laggard at 0% and 4.1%. The dealer/distributor and trading-house metrics do not differentiate peers in FY2022-23 because all values are missing and therefore ranked last under the defined rule. Tata’s FY2022-23 values also require caution because the dataset flags them as consolidated, including international operations, whereas most other company-years are standalone Indian operations. Overall, SAIL and JSW lead the theme quantitatively for FY2022-23, but this appears driven by product-information coverage methodology rather than necessarily superior product circularity. Jindal’s lag is disclosure-driven, especially given stainless steel’s inherent recyclability, which becomes visible in later-year disclosures.

**FY2023-24**
FY2023-24 shows a more balanced but bifurcated benchmark. JSW, SAIL, and Jindal Stainless all report 100% coverage for both product environmental/social information and recycling/safe-disposal information, while Tata remains materially below the leaders at 53% and 16%. However, Tata leads the openness-of-business channel indicators, with 1,176 dealers/distributors and zero trading houses, indicating broad sales reach and no trading-house purchase dependence under the reported definition. Jindal reaches 100% product-information coverage and 240 dealers/distributors, but its procurement model shows high intermediary exposure, with 500 trading houses and 78% purchases from trading houses. SAIL combines 100% product-information coverage with zero trading-house purchases, but has a smaller dealer/distributor base at 194. Therefore, product-responsibility disclosure leaders are JSW, SAIL, and Jindal, while Tata leads market-channel breadth and procurement-route simplicity. The year also marks Tata’s transition into more comparable disclosure after restatements and boundary changes.

**FY2024-25**
FY2024-25 is the primary benchmark year. Tata is strongest on channel reach and procurement transparency: it reports 1,661 dealers/distributors, 38% sales through dealers/distributors, and zero trading houses for purchases. JSW and Jindal lead the two product-information indicators at 100% each, while Tata remains materially behind at 52% for environmental/social product information and 17% for recycling/safe-disposal information. SAIL’s product-information indicators fall to 0%, but it retains zero trading-house dependence and reports 168 dealers/distributors. Jindal is the most improved integrated product-responsibility performer, combining 100% product-information coverage with 367 dealers/distributors and strong stainless-specific circularity claims, but it remains the weakest on trading-house dependence: 478 trading houses, 80% purchases from trading houses, and 62% top-10 trading-house concentration. For Tata senior management, the key gap is not circular-economy capability—Tata recycled about 4.2 Mt scrap in FY2024-25—but translation of that capability into full turnover-linked consumer/product information coverage.
